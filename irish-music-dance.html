Traditional Irish Music

Traditional Irish music, or "trad" as it's often called, is characterized by its lively melodies, distinctive rhythms, and rich ornamentation. Dating back centuries, this musical tradition has been passed down primarily through oral transmission, with each generation adding their own variations and interpretations. Irish music typically follows a structure of repeated patterns called "tunes," which include jigs, reels, hornpipes, and slow airs, each with its own distinctive rhythm and character.

Instruments

The traditional instruments of Irish music reflect both indigenous developments and cultural exchanges over centuries:

  • Uilleann Pipes - A complex, bellows-blown bagpipe unique to Ireland with a sweet, melodic sound
  • Fiddle - Identical to a violin but played in distinctive Irish styles with unique ornamentation
  • Irish Flute - A wooden, simple-system flute that produces a breathy, resonant tone
  • Tin Whistle - A simple six-holed instrument crucial to the Irish tradition
  • Bodhrán - A frame drum played with a double-headed stick called a "tipper" or "beater"
  • Concertina and Button Accordion - Free-reed instruments that became popular in the 19th century
  • Irish Harp - Ireland's national emblem and an ancient instrument with a distinctive triangular shape

Song Traditions

Irish vocal music spans several distinct traditions:

  • Sean-nós - A highly ornamented, unaccompanied singing style in the Irish language
  • Ballads - Narrative songs chronicling historical events, love stories, and emigration
  • Work Songs - Rhythmic songs that accompanied labor, from weaving to harvesting
  • Lullabies - Gentle cradle songs often containing references to ancient mythology

Session Culture

The Irish music session—an informal gathering of musicians in a pub or home—is the heartbeat of traditional music culture. Sessions follow unwritten etiquette regarding tune selection, joining in, and respect for more experienced players. This communal practice has preserved the tradition while allowing it to evolve naturally.

Rather than relying on written notation, traditional musicians learn by ear, developing a repertoire of hundreds of tunes through listening and practice. The emphasis is on individual interpretation while honoring the core melody, creating a dynamic tradition that is both stable and constantly evolving.

Irish Dance

Irish dance is characterized by a rigid upper body with rapid, precise movements of the feet. This distinctive style evolved partly in response to confined spaces and partly from historical circumstances. The tradition encompasses several distinct forms, each with their own techniques, contexts, and histories.

Sean-nós Dancing

Sean-nós (old style) is the oldest form of Irish step dancing, characterized by its improvisational nature, relaxed arms, close-to-the-floor steps, and rhythmic complexity. Traditionally performed in small spaces like kitchens or crossroads, this style emphasizes personal expression and rhythmic interpretation rather than standardized steps.

Regional variations developed in different parts of Ireland, with distinct styles emerging in Connemara, Cork, and Kerry. Sean-nós dancers typically perform solo, often on a small surface like a table top or a door taken off its hinges, focusing on creating intricate percussive rhythms with their feet.

Step Dancing

Competitive step dancing, formalized by the Gaelic League and later by the Irish Dancing Commission (CLRG), is characterized by its rigid upper body, straight arms, and precisely executed footwork. This is the style most recognized internationally, especially following the Riverdance phenomenon of the 1990s.

Competition dance includes both solo and figure dances, performed in soft shoes (ghillies) or hard shoes with fiberglass tips and heels that create distinctive sounds. Dancers progress through levels from beginner to championship, competing at feiseanna (competitions) locally and internationally, with the World Championships representing the pinnacle of achievement.

Competitive Irish step dancers in elaborate costumes performing at a feis (competition).

Céilí Dancing

Céilí dancing involves group formations and coordinated movements, similar to square dancing or contra dancing in other traditions. These social dances typically involve groups of four, eight, or sixteen dancers performing set patterns. Popular céilí dances include The Walls of Limerick, The Siege of Ennis, and The Haymakers Jig.

Originally performed at community gatherings called céilís (social events with music, dance, and storytelling), these dances were codified in the early 20th century by the Gaelic League as part of the Irish cultural revival. Unlike competitive step dancing, céilí dancing emphasizes participation and social interaction rather than technical virtuosity.

Historical Development

Ancient and Medieval Periods

Ancient Irish society held musicians in high regard, with evidence of musical practices dating back thousands of years. The most revered musicians were the harpers, who performed for nobility and enjoyed special legal status under Brehon Law. Early Irish literature contains numerous references to music and dance, including descriptions of ancient instruments and performance contexts.

Medieval Irish music was influenced by the arrival of Christianity and later Norman invasions, creating a blend of indigenous and imported elements. By the 16th century, travelers to Ireland were already commenting on the distinctive nature of Irish music and dance, noting the rapid footwork and characteristic held upper body that remains distinctive today.

A representation of a medieval Irish harper, based on historical descriptions and carvings.

18th and 19th Centuries

The 18th century saw itinerant dancing masters traveling from town to town, teaching dance steps to local communities and developing regional styles. These dancing masters were key cultural figures who refined and standardized many of the techniques still used today.

During the 19th century, instruments like the accordion and concertina were introduced to Ireland, changing the sound and repertoire of Irish music. The Great Famine (1845-1852) severely disrupted musical traditions, as millions died or emigrated, taking their musical knowledge with them but also spreading Irish music worldwide.

Revival Movements

The late 19th and early 20th centuries saw concerted efforts to preserve and revive Irish cultural expressions, including music and dance. The Gaelic Revival movement, led by organizations like the Gaelic League, sought to counter the decline of traditional art forms by establishing formal structures for their preservation and promotion.

In 1927, the Irish Dancing Commission (CLRG) was established to regulate competitions and standardize teaching methods. This led to greater uniformity in dance styles but also helped preserve the tradition when many cultural practices were being abandoned. Similarly, Comhaltas Ceoltóirí Éireann, founded in 1951, became instrumental in promoting traditional music through competitions, teaching, and recordings.

The folk revival of the 1960s and 1970s brought Irish music to new audiences, with groups like The Chieftains, Planxty, and The Bothy Band revitalizing traditional music for contemporary listeners. This period saw innovations in arrangement and instrumentation while maintaining core traditional elements.

Global Influence

Diaspora Impact

Irish emigrants carried their musical traditions to communities worldwide, particularly to the United States, Canada, Australia, and Britain. These diaspora communities often preserved aspects of traditional music and dance that evolved or disappeared in Ireland itself. The interaction between Irish music and other traditions led to new forms like Irish-American vaudeville acts and the development of distinctive regional styles.

In the United States, Irish music influenced the development of country, bluegrass, and American folk music, with instruments like the fiddle and dance tunes crossing cultural boundaries. Meanwhile, Irish dance schools established in cities with large Irish populations helped maintain cultural connections across generations.

Riverdance Phenomenon

The seven-minute Riverdance interval performance at the 1994 Eurovision Song Contest revolutionized global perceptions of Irish dance. Choreographed by Michael Flatley and Jean Butler, with music by Bill Whelan, this contemporary presentation of Irish dance captivated worldwide audiences and led to a full-length show that has been seen by over 25 million people.

Riverdance and subsequent shows like Lord of the Dance transformed Irish dance from a community tradition to an international performance art. This commercialization had complex effects—increasing interest in Irish dance globally while also influencing competitive styles toward more theatrical presentations with higher kicks and greater athleticism.

A Riverdance performance, showcasing the theatrical style that brought Irish dance to global audiences.

Modern Fusion

Contemporary Irish music has embraced innovations while maintaining connections to tradition. Artists like Eileen Ivers, Martin Hayes and Dennis Cahill, and groups like Lunasa and Dervish have incorporated elements from classical, jazz, rock, and world music traditions while respecting traditional forms.

Digital technology has transformed how Irish music is shared and learned, with online tutorials and social media connecting practitioners worldwide. While some traditionalists express concern about authenticity, this global network has created new vitality in the tradition, introducing Irish music and dance to diverse audiences and ensuring its continued evolution.

Irish dance has similarly evolved, with contemporary choreographers creating works that combine traditional steps with modern dance techniques. Companies like Fidget Feet incorporate aerial elements, while Colin Dunne and others have developed experimental approaches that question and expand the boundaries of Irish dance while honoring its essential characteristics.

Notable Figures

Music

  • Turlough O'Carolan (1670-1738) - Blind harper and composer whose melodies remain popular in the repertoire
  • Seán Ó Riada (1931-1971) - Composer who revolutionized Irish music in the mid-20th century
  • The Chieftains - Formed in 1962, this group led by Paddy Moloney brought Irish music to international audiences
  • Mary Bergin - Virtuoso tin whistle player known for her breathtaking technique
  • Martin Hayes - County Clare fiddle player renowned for his soulful, minimalist approach
  • Sharon Shannon - Innovative accordion player who has expanded the boundaries of traditional music

Dance

  • Michael Flatley - Dancer and choreographer who revolutionized Irish dance performance
  • Jean Butler - Original female lead in Riverdance who helped bring Irish dance to worldwide attention
  • Colin Dunne - Champion dancer and choreographer known for pushing artistic boundaries
  • Breandán de Gallaí - Former Riverdance principal who creates contemporary Irish dance works
  • Mick Mulkerrin and Mairéad Casey - Master sean-nós dancers who have preserved this older tradition

Experience Irish Music and Dance

To truly understand Irish music and dance, consider these experiences:

  • Attend a traditional session in an Irish pub
  • Visit major festivals like Fleadh Cheoil na hÉireann, The Willie Clancy Summer School, or Féile an Phobail
  • Take a workshop with a master musician or dancer
  • Explore regional styles by visiting areas like Clare, Donegal, or Connemara
  • Listen to archival recordings through the Irish Traditional Music Archive
